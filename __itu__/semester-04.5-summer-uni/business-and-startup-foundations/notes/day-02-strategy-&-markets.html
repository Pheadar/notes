On a correlative and evolutionary SWOT analysis

by Charis Vlados
#article #summary #swot
DOI 10.1108/JSMA-02-2019-0026

(GPT Summary)

Purpose:

The paper aims to propose a new approach to SWOT analysis that is more effective for strategic planning in contemporary organizations.

Critique of Conventional SWOT Analysis:

Conventional SWOT analysis is criticized for being static, overly simplistic, and not taking into account the dynamic nature of business environments.
It often treats strengths, weaknesses, opportunities, and threats as isolated and absolute, rather than interrelated and evolving.

Methodological Approach:

The new approach integrates concepts from evolutionary economics and the Stra.Tech.Man framework, which emphasizes the interconnectedness of strategy, technology, and management.

Correlative and Evolutionary SWOT Analysis:

This new method views opportunities and threats as potential and context-dependent, based on the organization's unique strengths and weaknesses.
It emphasizes that opportunities and threats are not the same for all organizations; they depend on the specific capabilities and circumstances of each organization.

Key Findings:

The correlative and evolutionary approach offers a more nuanced and dynamic understanding of the business environment.
It provides a framework for organizations to systematically compare their internal capabilities with external opportunities and threats.

Implications for Strategic Planning:

Organizations should focus on both internal and external environments in a comparative and evolutionary manner.
Strategic actions should aim to leverage comparative strengths to turn potential opportunities into real ones and mitigate comparative weaknesses to avoid potential threats.

  • Steps for Implementation:
    • Conduct a detailed analysis of the internal and external environments.
    • Identify and evaluate specific strengths, weaknesses, opportunities, and threats.
    • Develop strategic actions that align with the organization's unique context and evolutionary trajectory.

Examples and Case Studies:

The paper provides examples from various fields, including renewable energy, rural development, and waste management, to illustrate the application of the new SWOT analysis approach.

Conclusion:

The correlative and evolutionary SWOT analysis is presented as a more effective tool for strategic planning in today's complex and dynamic business environments.
Future research and development of this approach are necessary to refine its operational form and integrate quantitative and qualitative data.

Exercise: Porter's 5 Forces

#exercise #porters5forces

  • Vaccines:

    • Competitive Rivals:
      • Pfizer, Johnson&Johnson, AstraZeneca
      • Lots of them, very established, international
      • Market growth not so much, but will probably continue growing
      • Hard to differentiate product
      • Market exit barriers: maybe useful in terms of overlapping research for other medication
    • New competitors:
      • High market entry barriers (expensive material, specialized workforce...)
      • Standardized methods, research is publicly available, etc. Probably expensive to begin producing, but beyond that not a much bigger advantage if already established.
      • Hard to get into the distribution market due to approval and specific needs of transportation.
      • Maybe not so much anymore, white brand medication.
    • Buyers:
      • Probably more government than individual. National health services and so on.
      • None. Governments won't produce their own but subsidise research and production.
    • Substitutes:
      • Genetic engineering, Natural medicine
      • Not really many substitutes in the near-future
    • Suppliers:
      • Common materials
      • Standard and common equipment
      • Not much bargaining power

  • Dairy Products

    • Alternative milks, cheeses, etc (dairy product substitutes)
    • Lots of competitors in the market

Market Strategies

#markets #market-strategies

  • Generic Market Strategies
  • Ansoff's Product-Market Strategies
  • Portfolio Analysis

Generic Market Strategies

#strategy #market-strategies

  • Cost Leadership: lowest cost of all competitors
  • Differentiation: higher quality
  • Focus: specific segment/niche
  • Middle of the road: not successful if you try to do all of them. There has to be some specification on one or two of the previous strategies.

Ansoff's Product-Market Strategies

#strategy #market-strategies #ansoffs-product-market

Ansoff Matrix:

  • Market penetration: increase market activities (improve existing products), increase market share
  • Market development: enter new markets (use existing products to develop market potential)
  • Product development: increase sales in existing markets (new products)
  • Diversification: unlock new business area (everything new)

Portfolio Analysis

#portfolio-analysis #bcg-matrix

Goals:

  • Combine internal and external perspective
    • How market is evolving
    • Current position in the market
  • Most common one is the BCG (Boston Consulting Group) matrix:

Relative Market Share: Your Market Share divided by Market Share of strongest competitor (>1 = good)

4 mega-trends that could change the world by 2030

Chris Weller - World Economic Forum
#article #summary #trends

(GPT Summary)

  1. Individual Empowerment: Increased aid will lift millions out of poverty, leading to economic growth and more empowered individuals, but also potential risks from increased access to weapons.

  2. Diffusion of Power: Asian countries, especially China, will become dominant global powers, shifting economic influence from traditional Western nations.

  3. Demographic Changes: The global population will grow to 8.3 billion, becoming older and more urbanized, posing challenges to maintaining living standards and infrastructure.

  4. Rising Demand for Resources: Demand for food, water, and energy will increase significantly due to a growing middle class and climate change, necessitating careful resource management.

The World in 2030: Nine Megatrends to Watch

Andrew S. Winston - MIT Sloan Management Review
#article #summary #trends

(GPT Summary)

  1. Demographics: The global population will reach 8.5 billion, with a significant increase in the elderly population and the middle class, though wealth inequality will persist.

  2. Urbanization: Two-thirds of the global population will live in cities, necessitating more efficient infrastructure and urban agriculture.

  3. Transparency: Increased data collection and sharing will lead to a more open, but less private world.

  4. Climate Crisis: Climate change will bring extreme weather, rising sea levels, and biodiversity loss, stressing food and water resources and causing mass migrations.

  5. Resource Pressures: Growing demand for resources will push for more sustainable practices, such as circular economies and advanced water technologies.

  6. Clean Tech: Renewable energy and electric vehicles will dominate, supported by advancements in clean technologies and stricter regulations on fossil fuels.

  7. Technology Shifts: AI and the internet of things will become pervasive, transforming industries and jobs but also increasing manipulation and privacy concerns.

  8. Global Policy: The future of global cooperation is uncertain, with potential for both increased nationalism and collaborative efforts to address climate change and inequality.

  9. Populism: The rise of nationalism and radicalism may continue or wane, depending on societal responses to economic and technological changes.

Why Design Thinking Works

Jeanne Liedtka - Harvard Business Review
#article #summary #design

(GPT Summary)

Design thinking can drive innovation by addressing human biases and behaviors that hinder creativity. It compares design thinking to total quality management (TQM) in its potential to radically improve processes.

Key Points:

  1. Understanding Design Thinking:

    • Design thinking combines tools like ethnographic research, problem reframing, experimentation, and diverse teams.
    • It helps overcome biases such as a preference for the status quo and resistance to change.

  2. Benefits of Design Thinking:

    • Superior Solutions: Encourages innovative problem-solving by focusing on user needs and involving diverse perspectives.
    • Lower Risks and Costs: Promotes a portfolio approach to manage uncertainty, favoring experimentation over sticking with only safe, incremental ideas.
    • Employee Buy-In: Involving employees in the innovation process increases their commitment and support for new ideas.

  3. Structured Process:

    • Design thinking provides a structured, step-by-step process that guides teams through discovery, ideation, and prototyping.
    • This structure helps non-designers adapt to new behaviors and reduces the fear of failure.

  4. Customer Discovery:

    • Emphasizes immersion in the customer experience to uncover hidden needs, going beyond traditional data analysis.
    • Example: At the Kingwood Trust, understanding an autistic person's behavior led to designing environments that cater to their strengths and pleasures.

  5. Sense Making:

    • Uses tools like the Gallery Walk to organize and interpret data, facilitating shared insights and overcoming individual biases.
    • Example: Monash Health improved patient care by aligning on the true motivations behind their clinical practices.

  6. Idea Generation and Testing:

    • Encourages the generation of diverse ideas and rigorous testing through rough prototypes, allowing for iterative learning and refinement.
    • Example: Monash Health's "telecare" guides demonstrated success in reducing hospital admissions through real-world experiments.

  7. Social Technology:

    • Design thinking is described as a social technology that reshapes innovators' experiences, fostering collaboration and alignment at each step of the innovation process.
    • It addresses the human aspects of innovation, creating a culture of engagement, dialogue, and continuous learning.

Overall, the article highlights how design thinking's structured yet flexible approach can unleash creative potential, drive superior solutions, reduce risks, and secure broad-based support for innovation.

Lecture Notes

Part 1

#notes #megatrends

Megatrends

  • Driving forces that define the world today and towards the future.
  • Global, sustained and macroeconomic.
  • Influence activities, processes and perceptions

They matter because of their impact on the market, and have strong implications for the future of companies in terms of strategy, development and innovation processes.

Part 2

#notes #human-centered

Human Centred Approach

User interfaces and/or systems are designed to fit users, rather than the other way around (users fitting the systems).

The Human Centred Process:

  1. Inspiration
    • observe and understand
    • identify the problem
  2. Ideation
    • generate ideas
    • test and refine
  3. Implementation
    • idea to market
    • scale up

Double Diamond Model of Design Thinking

Problem Formulation

  • Who benefits?
  • When/how?
  • What are the desires and needs?
  • What are the difficulties?

Start with who you're solving the problem for, by creating a scenario/story. Get information and data on the situation/problem.

Stakeholders

  • Those who can affect or are affected by the process/idea/organization.
  • Can be individuals or groups.
  • They are key for the creation, feasibility, implementation, and sustainability of the process/idea.
